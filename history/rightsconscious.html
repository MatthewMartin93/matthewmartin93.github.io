Goals & Daily Struggles

Issues They Faced

The Draft

When men turned 18, they faced the life-changing threat of being drafted into the Vietnam War, a conflict many considered unjust and unwinnable.

Corporate Materialism

Young people saw employees trapped in a "rat race," choosing money and status over genuine human connection and meaningful lives.

Campus Control

Universities banned political speech and organizing, controlled dress codes, and locked dorms at night. These rules operated under in loco parentis ("in the place of a parent").

What They Demanded
1

End the War

An immediate halt to U.S. involvement in Vietnam, the most visible and visceral demand of the entire movement.

2

Participatory Democracy

A genuine political voice for young people, not just the right to vote at 21 but real representation and civic engagement at all levels.

3

Social Justice

An end to racism and inequality, deeply connected to and inspired by the Civil Rights Movement happening simultaneously.

4

Reject Conformity

Freedom to live, dress, think, and organize without institutional pressure to conform to postwar American normalcy.

02

Key Leaders

Student Organizer
Tom Hayden

Student at the University of Michigan and co-founder of the Students for a Democratic Society (SDS). Primary author of the Port Huron Statement, the defining manifesto of the New Left.

Free Speech Activist
Mario Savio

Philosophy student at UC Berkeley and central figure of the Berkeley Free Speech Movement. Galvanized students with fiery speeches calling for education to serve social justice.

Counterculture Icon
Timothy Leary

Harvard psychologist and prominent advocate for psychedelic substances. Coined the phrase "Turn on, tune in, drop out," a rallying call for rejecting mainstream society.

Musical Voice
Jimi Hendrix

Revolutionary guitarist whose electric sound became the sonic embodiment of the counterculture. Performed at Woodstock and represented the artistic freedom the movement championed.

Political Provocateurs
Hoffman & Rubin

Abbie Hoffman and Jerry Rubin co-founded the Youth International Party (Yippies), blending political theater with protest to mock and disrupt the political establishment.

Student Organization
Students for a Democratic Society

Founded in 1960, the SDS became the central organizational force of the student movement, organizing protests, drafting manifestos, and coordinating campus activism nationwide.

Key Events

June 15, 1962

The Port Huron Statement & the New Left

Manifesto

The Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) released their landmark manifesto, a sweeping critique of American society written primarily by Tom Hayden. In it they declared, "We are people of this generation, bred in at least modest comfort, housed now in universities, looking uncomfortably to the world we inherit... our comfort was penetrated by events too troubling to dismiss. First, the eroding and intolerable contradictions of total racism in American life... Second, the enclosing fact of the Cold War, symbolized by the presence of the Bomb." The statement attacked racial segregation, the arms race, corporate power, and Cold War complacency, calling on universities to become sites of "social critique and democratic experimentation." The document launched a broad political realignment that became known as the New Left.

1964–1965 Academic Year

The Berkeley Free Speech Movement

Campus Protest

At UC Berkeley, students erupted after the university banned political advocacy on a popular campus strip. On October 1, 1964, graduate student Jack Weinberg was arrested for running a civil rights table. Enraged students surrounded the police car and kept it blockaded for 32 hours while protesters climbed atop it to deliver speeches. Mario Savio rallied the movement, arguing that students had a duty to use their education to fight injustice. His most famous line insisted that when the machine becomes "so odious" students must "put your bodies upon the gears... and you've got to make it stop," framing direct action as a moral obligation. The ultimate result was historic: free speech and political organizing became recognized rights on college campuses nationwide.

January 14, 1967

The Human Be-In, San Francisco

Cultural Gathering

Over 20,000 people gathered in Golden Gate Park for a day-long celebration of peace and counterculture, organized by San Francisco artist Michael Bowen. Inspired partly by California's 1966 ban on LSD, Bowen aimed to unite hippies and Berkeley anti-war activists. A newspaper account described the arrival like a "gathering of the tribes," noting that "Beards had been curled, dyed, and decorated with flowers" and that costumes ranged from a red gunnysack to a flour sack stamped "U.S.A.", with "priests of some strange religion" and even a participant who arrived by parachute. The report captured how people arrived and acted at the event, publicly advocating peace, love, and LSD, themes that defined the era. The Human Be-In became a key gathering of like-minded people who wanted to express their views on peace and civil rights in public. Timothy Leary debuted his famous phrase "turn on, tune in, drop out" before the crowd for the first time. Bands including the Grateful Dead performed while attendees openly embraced the psychedelic lifestyle. To secure a permit, organizers falsely told the city it was a private birthday party, which ensured no police presence on the grounds.

August 15–18, 1969

Woodstock Music Festival

Defining Moment

Held on a farm in Bethel, New York, Woodstock drew an estimated 400,000 people, far surpassing its intended capacity, and became the defining cultural event of the entire counterculture era. Legendary performances by Jimi Hendrix, Janis Joplin, Jefferson Airplane, and The Who turned the festival into a symbol of the generation's ideals. Despite overwhelming logistical challenges, attendees largely maintained the spirit of peace and cooperation the movement championed, demonstrating that young Americans could gather en masse without violence.
